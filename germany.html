Current Subsidies

BEG — Federal Subsidy for Efficient Buildings (KfW 458)

Active since February 27, 2024 via KfW Program 458. Subsidies cover 30–70% of heat pump installation costs. Program guaranteed to continue until at least 2029, as confirmed in the CDU/CSU–SPD coalition agreement. Subsidy rates may be adjusted during this period. Additional Emissionsminderungszuschlag of €2,500 available — total max subsidy up to €23,500.

Base Rate

30%

Available to all homeowners replacing any existing heating system with a heat pump. No income requirements. Applies to all heat pump types including hybrids.

Speed Bonus (Klimageschwindigkeitsbonus)

+20%

For replacing a functioning fossil fuel heater that is 20+ years old. Bonus decreases by 3 percentage points every 2 years starting 2028.

Income Bonus (Einkommensbonus)

+30%

For households with a taxable annual income below €40,000. Can be combined with all other bonuses.

Efficiency Bonus

+5%

For heat pumps using natural refrigerants (propane, CO₂) or ground-source / water-source systems.

Maximum Combined Subsidy

Up to 70% — max €23,500

Maximum eligible costs: €30,000 for single-family homes. All bonuses stackable up to 70% (€21,000) + Emissionsminderungszuschlag €2,500.

Budget 2026
€11.96B
↓ €3.36B cut from 2025
Change from Jan 1, 2026

New Noise Requirements for Air-Source Heat Pumps

Outdoor units must now be at least 10 dB below legal limits (previously 5 dB). Heat pumps under 6 kW may not exceed 55 dB. This affects some older models that may no longer qualify for subsidies.

How to Apply

Application Process via KfW

All applications go through KfW (Kreditanstalt für Wiederaufbau). You must apply before starting any installation work.

1

Get a Quote

Hire a certified energy consultant (Energieberater). Get a detailed quote from a qualified installer (Fachunternehmen).

2

Apply Online

Submit your application through the KfW Zuschussportal before any work begins. Include the quote and energy consultant's report.

3

Receive Approval

Wait for the Zusage (approval letter) from KfW. Only then may installation begin. Processing typically takes 4–8 weeks.

4

Complete & Claim

After installation, submit invoices and proof of completion. KfW transfers the subsidy directly to your account.

Important: You must first sign a contract with a certified installer (Fachunternehmen) that includes a suspensive condition (aufschiebende Bedingung) tied to KfW approval. Apply online via Meine KfW Portal. Starting work before receiving the Zusage will void your eligibility.

Policy Changes & Signals

Recent developments that may affect heat pump subsidies and the heating market in Germany.

Critical
February 24, 2026
Heizungsgesetz (GEG) abgeschafft — ersetzt durch Gebäudemodernisierungsgesetz (GMG)
The federal government formally scrapped the GEG heating law and replaced it with the Building Modernisation Act (Gebäudemodernisierungsgesetz). The mandatory 65% renewable energy requirement for new heating systems has been dropped. Homeowners can now install oil and gas boilers without restrictions. Heat pump subsidies remain but the regulatory push for adoption is gone.

Municipal Heat Planning

Kommunale Wärmeplanung — Status Map

Municipal heat planning (Wärmeplanung) is a legal requirement for all German municipalities. It determines how each area will be heated in the future — district heating, heat pumps, hydrogen, or other solutions. The plan directly affects homeowners' obligations and subsidy eligibility.

What is it?

A legally mandated plan that maps out how each neighborhood will be heated in the future. The Wärmeplanungsgesetz (Heat Planning Act) came into force on January 1, 2024.

Why does it matter?

Once your municipality publishes its heat plan, it triggers specific obligations for homeowners in designated areas. If your area is marked for heat pumps — you'll need to switch. If marked for district heating — you may need to connect.

Deadlines

June 30, 2026 — cities with >100,000 residents
June 30, 2028 — all other municipalities
Plans must be publicly accessible.

Current Progress (as of early 2026)

Large cities (>100,000 residents) 63% started, 38% completed
Deadline: June 30, 2026 — 80 major municipalities
Medium cities (10,000–100,000) ~45% in progress
Deadline: June 30, 2028 — 1,518 municipalities
Small municipalities (<10,000) ~8% started
Deadline: June 30, 2028 — thousands of small municipalities
Challenges
  • Lack of financial resources in smaller municipalities
  • Personnel shortages for planning
  • 48% of municipalities have not formally begun
  • Political uncertainty after GEG abolition
What this means for homeowners
  • Check your municipality's status at your local Stadtwerk
  • If your area is planned for district heating — wait before installing HP
  • If planned for decentralized heating — heat pump is the best option
  • If no plan yet — installing a heat pump with BEG subsidy now is safe
Sources: BMWSB · BDEW · BBSR

Summary & Outlook

Where Is Germany Heading?

Assessment of the political direction, probability of further changes, and long-term market implications.

Change Probability Assessment
65% rule already scrapped Confirmed
Subsidy rate reduction Possible (2027–28)
Full subsidy cancellation Unlikely before 2029

Positive Signals

  • + Subsidies confirmed through at least 2029
  • + Market rebounding — 299k units in 2025 (+55%)
  • + BWP forecast for 2026: 410k–530k units
  • + Heat pump prices stabilizing / declining
  • + Government target: 6M heat pumps by 2030

Risk Factors

  • − GEG abolished — no regulatory push for HP adoption
  • − BEG budget cut by €3.36B in 2026
  • − CSU pushing to end "excessive" subsidies
  • − Gas/oil boilers allowed again without restrictions
  • − Political shift toward "technology openness"

Long-Term Market Outlook

Germany is entering a policy paradox: the government maintains generous heat pump subsidies (up to 70%) while simultaneously removing the regulatory framework that drove adoption. The abolition of the GEG's 65% renewable heating rule eliminates the most powerful market driver — mandatory compliance.

In the short term (2026–2027), the market will likely continue growing due to locked-in subsidies, municipal heat planning deadlines (Wärmeplanung), and improving economics. The BWP's forecast of 410,000 units in 2026 appears achievable. However, the removal of regulatory urgency combined with budget cuts creates a ceiling effect — growth may slow after the initial recovery.

The critical period is 2027–2028, when the speed bonus begins declining and the coalition may revisit subsidy levels. If gas prices remain low and no new regulatory pressure emerges, the government's target of 500,000 annual installations and 6 million heat pumps by 2030 will be extremely difficult to achieve.

Bottom line: For consumers, the current subsidy window (2026–2028) represents the best financial opportunity. For the industry, diversifying beyond the German market and investing in cost reduction will be essential to sustain growth in a less supportive policy environment.
